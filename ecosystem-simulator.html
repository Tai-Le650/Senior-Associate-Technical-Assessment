Ecosystem Simulator

An educational sandbox game where players paint terrain, plants, and animals into a live 3D world and watch the ecosystem unfold.

Play the game ↗

00 About this Game

An educational, web-based sandbox game built in Godot 4.6 with GDScript, where players draw terrains and place plants and animals to see how their choices shape an ecosystem over time. It includes multiple game modes — Free Play, Pre-Lab, Walk-Through simulation, and Explore Planets — plus an AI helper bot with text and speech support, as well as post-simulation analysis, to help high school and undergraduate students learn STEM concepts. Quizzes at the end of Pre-Lab and Walk-Through, simulation scrubbing, and a 3D sandbox round it out.

01 How to Play

Start by picking a game mode from the lobby: Free Play if you want to experiment freely, Walk-Through if you want a guided lesson, Pre-Lab if you want to follow the scientific method, or Explore Planets if you want to load a real-world location. Once you’re in the sandbox, use the Build Tools panel on the left to select a terrain type, plant, or animal, then click or drag on the 3D canvas to paint your ecosystem. Right-click and drag to rotate the camera, and scroll to zoom in or out.

When your ecosystem looks the way you want, hit Play and watch your animals hunt, eat, drink, reproduce, and die in real time. You can drag the timeline slider to rewind and see what happened at any point, then resume from there to explore a different outcome. When you’re done, click End Simulation to get a breakdown of your ecosystem’s health and an AI-written analysis of what worked and what didn’t.

If you ever get stuck, the Tutorial button in the header walks you through the current mode step by step, and the Helper Bot (💬) can answer questions at any time.

02 Game Features

Free Play
Open sandbox where you build and run any ecosystem you want with no restrictions or goals.
Pre-Lab
Structured mode where you form a hypothesis, run a simulation, write a Claim–Evidence–Reasoning reflection, and take a quiz.
Walk-Through
Step-by-step guided lessons led by EcoBot across four ecology topics, with voice narration and a quiz at the end.
Explore Planets
Spin a 3D globe, click any real-world location, and load its actual climate and species into the sandbox.
3D Sandbox
Paint terrain, plants, and animals onto a live 3D world and watch them interact through a real ecology simulation.
Timeline Scrubbing
Drag a slider to rewind the simulation to any earlier moment, then resume from there to explore a different outcome.
AI Ecosystem Analysis
After every run, an AI reviews your ecosystem and explains what was healthy, what was off-balance, and why.
Helper Bot
A context-aware chat assistant available at any time that knows what mode you’re in and what’s happening in your simulation.
Voice Input & Output
Talk to the Helper Bot or answer reflection prompts with your microphone, and have EcoBot’s messages read aloud.
Ecosystem Metrics
Live tracking of biodiversity, food web balance, water quality, soil health, carbon storage, and erosion safety.

04 Creation Process

Before coding the game, I wrote down all the features I wanted and brainstormed how I wanted the game to run and function. I then put the same prompt into ChatGPT 5.2, Gemini 3, and Claude Sonnet 4.5 to compare the results. The prompt was:

Create a web-based HTML game where the STEM topic is Ecology / Environmental Science. Have it where the player starts off with an environment, and they can drag and drop different plants and animals. They can adjust the physical environment too — adding sand, rocks, trees, rivers, lakes, oceans, hills, and mountains. Each time the player adds or changes something it will show the effects of it in the game.

ChatGPT 5.2 produced the best-looking game that ran smoothly and had a solid feature set, so I used it to help me code an HTML/CSS/JavaScript version. Once that version was close to completion, I started thinking of additional features I wanted to add. I researched tradeoffs and brainstormed with Google Gemini Pro, and concluded that staying on plain HTML/CSS/JavaScript would make the new features too hard to implement — so I ported the game to the Godot game engine. Once I was on Godot, I switched to Claude Opus 4.5, 4.6, and 4.7 for code assistance.

05 Key Takeaways

I could have stored API keys inside Godot via an .env file and injected them as environment variables when deploying to the web, but that path was harder and more brittle than necessary — a backend turned out to be both more secure and simpler to implement.

To avoid AI hallucinations in the ecosystem analyses and lesson content, I anchored the model on real-world data so all facts and details stay accurate.

And a hard-earned lesson: some features that worked perfectly locally in Godot did not always behave the same way (or work at all) once deployed to the web, so testing the web build early and often was essential.
